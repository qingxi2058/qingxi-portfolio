Why creators are leaving ReelShort in 2026

ReelShort (parent: Crazy Maple Studio, backed by China's COL Group) is still the category leader — but 2026 has exposed three structural problems that are pushing creators and studios to alternatives.

The core issue: ReelShort's contract model was built for low-budget live-action in 2022–2024. AI-native creators in 2026 can deliver equivalent quality at 5–10% of the cost, and they don't need a publisher middleman.
  • Revenue share is punitive. Creators report 10–20% of net revenue, versus 55% on YouTube Shorts ad revenue and 100% if you own the distribution channel.
  • IP ownership transfers on submission. Most ReelShort licensing deals include perpetual worldwide exploitation rights — once you sign, you cannot take the title elsewhere.
  • AI adoption is slow internally. ReelShort still books Eastern European and US-based actors for principal photography. Their AI pilots (dubbing, face-retention) are post-production only, which means they're missing the 90% cost cut that pure-AI pipelines now deliver.

Meanwhile, industry reporting suggests Holywater's AI-native rival MyMuse is producing 100+ AI short dramas per month at roughly 5% of ReelShort's per-episode cost. The economics have flipped.

Are ReelShort scripts protected by copyright?

Yes — if you license a script to ReelShort, the original IP generally remains yours, but ReelShort holds exclusive exploitation rights for the term of the agreement (often 3–5 years, sometimes perpetual). You cannot take the same script to a competitor during that term.
